My CISSP Journey: Passing at 100 Questions

Published on January 2026

Introduction

CISSP is often considered a managerial certification, but my experience proves that a hands-on security engineer can clear it with the right mindset. I passed the CISSP exam at 100 questions with nearly 70 minutes remaining. This blog documents my background, preparation strategy, exam experience, and the lessons I learned along the way.

Background

I work as a security generalist and have experience across cloud security, SIEM implementation, DevSecOps, risk assessment, compliance, and incident response. While I had strong hands-on exposure to many CISSP domains, areas like networking, physical security, and asset security were completely new to me.

I have 3.5 years of full-time experience and 1 year of internship experience, totaling approximately 4.5 years. I also hold certifications including CompTIA Security+, AWS Security Specialty, Google Professional Cloud Security Engineer, and Microsoft Azure Security Engineer. With the one-year experience waiver from AWS Security Specialty, I meet the CISSP experience requirement.

Motivation and When I Started

I was initially motivated to pursue CISSP by a colleague from my previous company. Later, my current manager, who also holds CISSP, strongly motivated and supported me throughout the journey.

While I did not start serious preparation immediately, I was passively consuming CISSP-related content from February 2025, spending around one to two hours per week.

The real preparation started in August 2025, when I purchased the official CISSP CBK and official practice tests.

Study Resources

Official CISSP CBK

I started with the official CISSP CBK but never completed it. The language felt dense and difficult to follow. I primarily used it as a reference rather than a primary learning source.

LinkedIn Learning - Mike Chapple

This was the most impactful resource in my preparation. The course explained concepts clearly and aligned perfectly with the CISSP mindset. I completed the entire course in about two weeks.

Andrew Ramdayal

I purchased Andrew Ramdayal's CISSP course but completed only around ten percent of it. However, his CISSP mindset videos were extremely valuable and helped reinforce how to approach exam questions.

Practice Tests

Practice tests played the most important role in building my confidence.

  • Official CISSP practice tests including all domain-wise questions and four full-length exams
  • LearnZApp ISC2 Official CISSP App (all free questions)
  • Destination CISSP App (all free questions)
  • LinkedIn Learning full-length practice exams with consistent scores around eighty percent

Exam Day Experience

I appeared for the exam at the Gurugram test center in India. Since there was no CISSP test center available in Rajasthan, I had to travel nearly six hundred kilometers.

The exam environment was professional and strict. The questions were scenario-based and required careful reading. Most questions focused on decision-making rather than direct technical knowledge.

The 100 Question Moment

When the exam suddenly ended at 100 questions and the survey appeared, I panicked. In the CISSP CAT format, stopping at 100 questions can mean either a very good or very bad outcome.

After completing the survey, I collected my belongings and waited for the result. When the Pearson VUE staff handed me the printed result with "Congratulations" on it, it was an unforgettable moment.

CISSP Mindset and Exam Strategy

CISSP is all about mindset. Focus on keywords like BEST, FIRST, and PRIMARY. Always prioritize process over technology and follow the order Prevent, Detect, Correct.

Example question logic:

Policy implemented in email security solution User training EDR Device encryption

In real life, you would apply multiple controls. In the CISSP exam, you must choose the single best preventive control. In this example, user training is the best answer.

Final Thoughts

I am a hands-on security engineer and not a traditional manager. However, CISSP does not require you to be a manager. It requires you to understand how systems and companies actually work.

If you understand how systems and a company operate end to end, you can think like a manager.
